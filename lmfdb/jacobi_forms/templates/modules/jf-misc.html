For a given lattice index $L$ of rank $n$ and character $\varepsilon^h$, the spaces $J_{k,L}(\varepsilon^h)$ are zero unless $k\equiv \frac h2 \bmod \Z$. Among these weights the first weight $s$ which is greater than or equal to the singular weight of $L$ is $$ s = \left\lceil \frac {n-h}2 \right\rceil + \frac h2, \quad\text{i.e.}\qquad s = \begin{cases} \frac n2&\text{if $n\equiv h\bmod 2$,}\\ \frac {n+1}2&\text{otherwise.} \end{cases} $$ We set $$ J_{\textrm{even},L}(\varepsilon^h) = \bigoplus_{k\equiv s\bmod 2\Z} J_{k,L}(\varepsilon^h), \qquad J_{\textrm{odd},L}(\varepsilon^h) = \bigoplus_{k\equiv s+1\bmod 2\Z} J_{k,L}(\varepsilon^h). $$ Both spaces are invariant under multiplication by elliptic modular forms on the full modular group $\mathrm{SL}(2,\Z)$. In fact, one can show that both are free modules of finite rank over the ring of elliptic modular forms over the full modular group. Recall that the ring of modular forms on the full modular group is a polynomial ring in two variables, generated by the forms $$ E_4 = 1-240\sum_{n\ge 1}\sigma_3(n)\,q^n, \qquad E_6 = 1+504\sum_{n\ge 1}\sigma_5(n)\,q^n. $$

As a consequence the Hilbert-Poincaré series $\dim J_{\_,L}(\varepsilon^h)$ of the two $\C[E_4,E_6]$-modules of Jacobi forms are of the form $$ \dim J_{\textrm{even},L}(\varepsilon^h) = \sum_{k\ge 0}\dim J_{s+2k,L}(\varepsilon^h) x^{s+2k} = x^s \frac {p_{\textrm{even}}(x)}{(1-x^4)(1-x^6)}, \qquad \dim J_{\textrm{odd},L}(\varepsilon^h) = \sum_{k\ge 0}\dim J_{s+1+2k,L}(\varepsilon^h) x^{s+2k} = x^{s+1} \frac {p_{\textrm{odd}}(x)}{(1-x^4)(1-x^6)}. $$ We call $p_{\textrm{even}}(x)$ and $p_{\textrm{odd}}(x)$ the Hilbert-Poincaré polynoimials of $J_{\textrm{even},L}(\varepsilon^h)$ and $J_{\textrm{odd},L}(\varepsilon^h)$, respectively.

It is clear that $p_{\textrm{even}}(x)$ and $p_{\textrm{odd}}(x)$ are even polynomials. It can be shown that their degrees satisfy $$\deg p_{\textrm{even/odd}}(x) \le 10.$$ If $p_{\textrm{even}}(x)=a(10)x^{10}+a(8)x^8+\cdots + a(2)x^2+a(0)$, then it easy to see that in any basis of $J_{\textrm{even},L}(\varepsilon^h)$ over $\C[E_4,E_6]$ the coefficient $a(2k)$ equals the number of generators of the given basis whose weights equal $s+2k$. In particular, the sum of the coefficients of $p_{\textrm{even}}(x)$ equals the rank of $J_{\textrm{even},L}(\varepsilon^h)$. A similar remark applies to $J_{\textrm{odd},L}(\varepsilon^h)$ and $p_{\textrm{odd}}(x)$.
